As a senior cybersecurity researcher at NIST's Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), I lead a research team tracking the cyber offense capabilities of AI systems through pre- and post-deployment model evaluations. I also support a team assessing vulnerabilities in AI systems. Working with leading AI labs and U.S. government partners, my work advances understanding of AI's national security implications and strengthens the resilience of these critical technologies.

Previously, I spent a decade at MIT Lincoln Laboratory in the Cyber System Assessments Group, where I led a research team at the intersection of dynamic program analysis, firmware security, and vulnerability discovery. In that role, I helped define the field of firmware rehosting, developed open-source tools, and applied them to evaluate the security of critical systems. This foundation in systems security evaluation and testing methodology now shapes how I approach AI: treating models themselves as complex, attackable systems.

I earned my PhD in Computer Science from Northeastern University and BS from Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, where I was an active member of RPISEC and its cyber Capture the Flag (CTF) team. I'm also passionate about cybersecurity education, having developed courses for universities, government agencies, and private companies. The materials from my System Security with Dynamic Program Analysis course are publicly available.

Projects and Publications

PENGUIN ▼

📄🔓 Publication + Open Source
Target-centric firmware rehosting that derives a per-image configuration to recreate the hardware/software environment and enable scalable dynamic analysis.
Python QEMU Firmware Rehosting Embedded Linux Dynamic Analysis

What it is: Instead of a one-size-fits-all recipe, PENGUIN automatically proposes and maintains a *configuration* (not code) describing the transformations a specific firmware needs (filesystem prep, pseudofiles/NVRAM, dummy NICs, VPNguin bridging) and ships analysis plugins to surface rehosting failures.

Why it mattered: On a corpus of 13,649 images (69 vendors; 8 architectures), PENGUIN produced workable rehosting environments at a substantially higher rate than prior systems (e.g., FirmAE), and includes a minimizer to find essential transformations.

HYDE ▼

📄🔓 Publication + Open Source
Hypervisor Dissociative Execution—inject and orchestrate guest syscalls from the VMM to *program* unmodified VMs for monitoring, management, and security.
Hypervisors System-call Injection Kernel Programming C/C++ VM Introspection

What it is: HyDE exposes an event-oriented interface that co-opts guest processes via targeted syscall injection, enabling "HyDE programs" (e.g., dynamic SBOM, password reset, 2FA-root) without in-guest agents or full VMI. Paper shows negligible overhead for one-off tasks and low overhead for persistent monitors.

Why it mattered: Demonstrates a practical middle-ground between agents and VMI that scales across Linux kernels by leaning on the syscall ABI as a stable contract. Code and SDK are open-sourced.

Publications

PANDA.re ▼

📄🔓 Publication + Open Source
Whole-system dynamic analysis platform on top of QEMU with deterministic record/replay, taint tracking, and a rich C/C++/Python plugin ecosystem.
C/C++ Python QEMU Record/Replay Dynamic Taint Analysis VM Introspection

What it is: PANDA (Platform for Architecture-Neutral Dynamic Analysis) extends QEMU with low-level callbacks, record/replay, and reusable analysis plugins (e.g., taint2, syscalls2, OSI) for whole-system reverse engineering, malware analysis, and crash triage.

Why it mattered: The PyPANDA paper introduced a Python 3 interface that unifies guest orchestration and analysis code, dramatically lowering the barrier to building whole-system analyses without sacrificing performance.

Rode0day & LAVA ▼

📄🔓 Publication + Open Source
LAVA automatically injects realistic vulnerabilities; Rode0day used these (and others) in a blind bug-finding competition to evaluate tools and approaches at scale.
Evaluating Cyber Capabilities Dynamic Taint Analysis Vulnerability Injection Benchmarks

What it is: LAVA uses PANDA taint to identify dataflow into dangerous sinks and inject reproducible "DUA"-based bugs. Rode0day ran recurring blind evaluations, releasing challenge corpora and results for reproducible study.

Why it mattered

  • Recognition: LAVA received a 2020 R&D 100 Award through MIT Lincoln Laboratory.
  • Impact: 733k CPU-hours across 8 fuzzers/20 targets; compares LAVA and other synthetic/hand-injected bugs. First blind evaluation platform providing realistic assessment of tool effectiveness.

SoK: Firmware Rehosting ▼

📄 Publication
Field-defining systematization that builds a taxonomy, terminology, and evaluation criteria for firmware rehosting research.
Firmware Analysis Survey Research IoT Security Embedded Systems

What it is: Establishes the scope of whole-system vs. component-level rehosting, discusses MMIO vs. OS-level abstractions, and sets out a comparative framework that subsequent work (incl. PENGUIN) builds on.

Why it mattered: First comprehensive systematization of firmware rehosting research that defined terminology and evaluation criteria for the field. Widely cited work that influenced the development of next-generation rehosting tools.

Homo in Machina ▼

📄 Publication
Guides human-in-the-loop fuzzing with *compartment analysis*—a dominator-based, dynamic-signal-aware method to prioritize large, under-covered regions.
Fuzzing Compartment Analysis Human-in-the-loop Code Coverage

What it is: Shows stable compartment rankings early in campaigns and coverage gains on OSS-Fuzz targets vs. AFL++, illustrating how analysis can focus manual effort where it most helps.

Why it mattered: Demonstrates how program analysis can inform fuzzing strategies, leading to improved coverage and more effective vulnerability discovery in complex software systems through human guidance.
